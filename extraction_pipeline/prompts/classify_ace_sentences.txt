CATEGORIES (use the exact label as the comment_tag):

1. L1: Elemental and encoded properties
   Enumerating visualization construction properties: chart type, marks, encoding channels, titles, axis labels, axis ranges, legends, colors, gridlines.
   The sentence must reference a visual element (axis, color, shape, grid, legend, label, etc.) OR describe the mapping between a visual encoding and the data variable it represents.
   **Grounding requirement:** The visual element or encoding must be consistent with the VISUALIZATION CONTEXT above. If it doesn't match the actual chart, reclassify based on what the sentence is actually doing.
   Examples: "The graph has a y-axis." "The x-axis is labeled in years." "There are tiered color blocks on the graph." "Each tiered color block symbolizes a different fuel for electricity."

2. L2: Statistical concepts and relations
   Reporting specific data values, descriptive statistics, extrema, outliers, correlations, or point-wise comparisons read from the chart.
   The sentence must cite a specific number, percentage, magnitude, or make a comparison between exactly two data points.
   **Grounding requirement:** The data value or comparison must be consistent with the variables and encodings in the VISUALIZATION CONTEXT above. If it doesn't match, reclassify.
   Examples: "Less than or about 25% of men visit their parents in person at least once a day." "The top 0.1% have a benefit that is up to 7 times the average." "Women are 20% more likely to use text or video-chat compared to men."

3. L3: Trend and pattern analysis
   Identifying complex trends, synthesizing patterns across multiple data points, noting exceptions, or conveying the overall gist of the visualization.
   The sentence describes a direction, shape, or overall pattern (e.g., "increasing", "skewed", "dominated by") — it captures what the visualization "shows" at a holistic level rather than reporting individual data points.
   **Grounding requirement:** The trend or pattern must be consistent with the VISUALIZATION CONTEXT above. If it doesn't match, reclassify.
   Examples: "Over time, there has been an overall steady increase in clean gigawatt hours." "China's graph shows a very steep increase in electricity generation." "Admissions are heavily skewed in favor of high-income legacy applicants."

4. Background knowledge
   A standalone factual claim about the world not directly shown in the chart, stated without connecting it causally to chart observations.
   Examples: "The Lehman Shock happened in 2008." "Fossil fuels contribute to climate change." "Society typically encourages young men to be independent."

5. Personal/episodic retrieval
   Self-reference, community reference, personal memory, or lived association.
   Examples: "I live in Akita." "My town has many factories." "When I was in junior high..." "I have a very close relationship with my parents."

6. Explanatory inference
   Causal or abductive reasoning that links a chart observation to a cause or mechanism. The sentence proposes WHY something in the data happened, using language like "because", "may explain", "relates to", "driven by", "due to".
   Examples: "The 2008 dip relates to the financial crisis." "Population may explain the increase." "Traditional gender stereotypes may affect how often a young person reaches out."

7. Predictive / counterfactual inference
   Future-oriented or hypothetical reasoning — what WILL or WOULD happen.
   Examples: "If fossil fuels remain steady, warming will worsen." "Clean energy may dominate later."

8. Evaluative / affective judgment
   Normative stance, concern, alarm, hope, moral reaction — a value judgment or emotional response.
   Examples: "This is a problem." "This increase is alarming." "This data gives me comfort."

9. Information need / curiosity
   Explicit question, expressed uncertainty, or desire for missing explanation.
   Examples: "I wonder why the trend reversed." "I ask whether this applies to other countries."

10. Meta / paratext
    Commentary about the graph artifact, its source, or the task — not about the world the data describes.
    Examples: "The headline is…" "Ember published the graph." "The graph gives more insight…"

11. Uncategorizable
    The sentence does not fit any of the above, or the ACE conversion has rendered it too ambiguous or malformed to classify meaningfully.
    Examples: "Something something." "It is." "The thing is interesting somehow."

DECISION PROCEDURE — apply these steps in order to select the category:

Step 1: Is the sentence about the visualization artifact itself (chart title, source, publisher, task)?
  → Yes → Meta / paratext (10)

Step 2: Is the sentence describing something visible IN the chart?
  → Yes → continue to Steps 2a–2c
  → No  → go to Step 3

  Step 2a: Does it name a visual element or encoding (axis, color, legend, mark, label, chart type)?
    → Yes → L1: Elemental and encoded properties (1)
  Step 2b: Does it cite a specific data value, number, or comparison between exactly two data points?
    → Yes → L2: Statistical concepts and relations (2)
  Step 2c: Does it synthesize a trend, overall pattern, or holistic gist across multiple data points?
    → Yes → L3: Trend and pattern analysis (3)

Step 3: Is it a personal anecdote, memory, or self-reference?
  → Yes → Personal/episodic retrieval (5)

Step 4: Is it primarily a normative stance, emotional reaction, or value judgment?
  → Yes → Evaluative / affective judgment (8)

Step 5: Is it an explicit question, expression of uncertainty, or desire for information?
  → Yes → Information need / curiosity (9)

Step 6: Does it propose WHY something happened — linking an observation to a cause?
  → Yes → Explanatory inference (6)

Step 7: Does it state a fact about the world not shown in the chart (without causal linking)?
  → Yes → Background knowledge (4)

Step 8: Is it future-oriented, hypothetical, or counterfactual?
  → Yes → Predictive / counterfactual inference (7)

Step 9: None of the above → Uncategorizable (11)

DISAMBIGUATION RULES for commonly confused pairs:

- L2 vs. L3: If the sentence cites a specific number or compares exactly two data points, prefer L2. If it describes a direction, shape, or overall pattern (e.g., "increasing", "skewed", "dominated by") without citing specific values, prefer L3.
- L3 vs. Explanatory inference: If the sentence only describes WHAT the chart shows (a pattern), it is L3. If it proposes WHY the pattern exists (invoking a cause), it is Explanatory inference — even if the pattern is mentioned in the same sentence.
- Background knowledge vs. Explanatory inference: If the sentence states a standalone fact without connecting it to chart data, it is Background knowledge. If it links that fact to an observation (even implicitly with "may", "because", "relates to"), it is Explanatory inference.
- Evaluative vs. Explanatory inference: If the sentence primarily expresses a value judgment or emotional reaction, prefer Evaluative. If it primarily proposes a cause, prefer Explanatory — even if the tone is evaluative.
- Background knowledge vs. Personal/episodic retrieval: If the sentence is about the self, one's community, or a personal memory, it is Personal/episodic. If it states a general fact about the world, it is Background knowledge.
